Australian Immigration

Australia is a migrant nation shaped by successive waves of arrival, settlement and cultural exchange. This data story follows how immigration has changed over time, where people come from, and how new communities are distributed across the country.

Past trend - The historic path to a multicultural Australia

The earliest population records that include country of birth show that 32% of Australia's population was born overseas in 1891, reflecting high migration in the late 19th century. This share fell to 10% by 1947 after World War I, the Great Depression and World War II slowed migration. It then rose rapidly during the post-war migration period before increasing more steadily from the 1970s onward.

Australia usually receives more migrants than it loses, so net overseas migration is an important contributor to population growth. The overseas-born share declined in 2021 as COVID-19 travel restrictions disrupted movement into and out of the country. After restrictions were removed in 2022, overseas migration increased again, led especially by overseas-born people arriving in Australia.

Modern-day statistics - Australian immigration seen from a global perspective

By total immigrant population, Australia sits among the world's leading destination countries, at a similar scale to countries such as France and Italy. When adjusted for population size, Australia ranks even higher, closer to countries such as Canada and Spain.

India, the United Kingdom and China account for more than one-third of Australia's overseas-born population, followed by the Philippines and New Zealand.

The immigrants - The human aspects that form Australian cultures

Australia's overseas-born population is strongly represented by young adults, especially people aged 18 to 30. Peaks around student and early-career ages suggest two major pathways into Australia: higher education and post-graduation work.

Most permanent migrants receive permanent status within about two to five years. Humanitarian entrants are more concentrated in the three-to-five-year range, suggesting a slightly different settlement pathway compared with other visa groups.

Among permanent migrants, skilled visa holders have the highest share of formal qualifications, while humanitarian entrants have the lowest. Qualification levels are balanced across gender, with bachelor degrees and Certificate III/IV credentials among the most common outcomes.

State-level statistics - How different parts of Australia affect immigration

Most overseas-born residents settle in Australia's five largest states, reflecting the pull of major labour markets, education hubs and established migrant communities. The state distribution shows that immigration is not spread evenly: it is closely connected to where jobs, universities, housing networks and long-standing migrant communities are concentrated.

Migrants from Asia, the Middle East, Eastern Europe and Africa are more likely to settle in New South Wales or Victoria, the states containing Australia's two largest cities. Migrants from New Zealand, the United Kingdom and Western Europe are more evenly spread across Queensland, South Australia and Western Australia.
